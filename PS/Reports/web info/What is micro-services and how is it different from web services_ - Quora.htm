Before you delve into microservices. I Recommend that you read up on Domain Driven Design.

Patterns, Principles And Practices Of Domain-driven Design

References: Microservice Architecture

Micro Service Architecture (MSA) can be seen as a light weight SOA.

Both architecture are about breaking down the monolith backends for greater scalability and maintainability of high traffic and complex web services. While SOA is more enterprise oriented, MSA is simpler in the sense it is easier to do service integration. The monolith backend is partioned into various services, sometimes as many as hundreds of services using the single responsibility principle. Each service has a buonded context, having its own datamodel and its own DB. The services communicate with each other through REST/JSON.

The services can be containerized using docker, or provisioned into a VM. The major difference is how the communication happen between the services. SOA relied on ESB. Also as the name implies, the services have a much more localized context.

The advantage of MSA is that the services can be scaled independently of other services depending on the load. Also when a service goes down, that does not bring down the entire system.

To understand the difference lets look at service and than microservice

What Is a Service?

Software service is very close to the Oxford American Dictionary (nontechnical) definition of any service, in that it is “a system supplying a public need.” Software services should serve the needs of one or more internal or external client applications. The interface of such services should therefore be designed from a client perspective, optimizing for maximum usefulness for its API consumer(s). While ideally every service is different as it optimizes for its clients’ use cases, it can generally be said that a service consists of a piece of functionality and its associated set of data. If the service is also the authoritative and definitive source for the current state of the data it holds and vends, then we call such a service a system of record. An examples of services where this system-of-record test does not pass could be a service that aggregates data and functionality from other services, which in turn could themselves be the systems of record for this data (for example, in an attempt to improve the usability or performance of the entire system).

Amazon CTO Werner Vogels describes as a general “service orientation” what comes very close to definition of microservices:

“For us service orientation means encapsulating the data with the business logic that operates on the data, with the only access through a published service interface. No direct database access is allowed from outside the service, and there’s no data sharing among the services."

Former eBay and Google executive Randy Shoup summarizes microservice characteristics as having

three main features:

• They have a very focused scope that concentrates on a very few functionalities (at times a single one), expressed by a small and also well-defined API.

• They are all very modular, and independently deployable, essentially elevating the rules of software encapsulation and modularity from the program to the deployment unit level.

• Finally, micro-services have isolated persistence, meaning that they do not share persistent storage, such as a common database.

Given the similarities to the earlier description of an SOA, how are micro-services really different? Is the term just a more modern, trendier name for the same service-oriented architecture? The main difference is that micro-services have the common trait of decomposing a large application into a set of small, focused, versioned, and loosely-coupled services, which are very inexpensive to spin up and maintain.

An excellent definition of micro-services is that of Fowler and Lewis, as an architectural style that

"… is an approach to developing a single application as a suite of small services, each running in its own process and communicating with lightweight mechanisms, often an HTTP resource API. These services are built around business capabilities and independently deployable by fully automated deployment machinery. There is a bare minimum of centralized management of these services, which may be written in different programming languages and use different data storage technologies."

Bhagwati Malav, Worked on microservices architecture
Answered Oct 23, 2017

These both fall under soa(Service oriented architecture). When you talk about micro-services you try diving your monolithic architecture into small services based on domains. It allows you to work independently on these small services, you should also be able to test and deploy without any problem. Lets say you have one ecommerce application. So we will understand difference between web service and micro-services here using this. If you writing web service it might be doing too many things in your application. But if you following micro-services architecture, you will have separate services for payments, search, recommendation, members etc. Thats how you need to divide whole application into loosely coupled, independent micro-service architecture. Let say you want to scale your payment, you can run multiple instances of this service, and keep running other services instances. You have freedom to choose your choice of language(java, scala etc.) db(postgres, mongodb etc.) But at the same time if you using web service or anything which is tightly coupled you wont have this flexibility in your applicaion. And if you want to make small changes in member side you don’t need to redeploy everything , just test this service and deploy. But at the same time if you are following monolithic way, you can’t do this.

Sahiti Kappagantula, Research Analyst @Edureka
Answered Apr 27

Microservices is a new software architecture. It is based on web services. But it can be any service implemented as an independent feature that has its own database and can be deployed independently.

Microservices is an architecture wherein all the components of the system are put into individual components, which can be built, deployed, and scaled individually.

Let me explain you with a simple analogy.

You must have seen how bees build their honeycomb by aligning hexagonal wax cells. They initially start with a small section using various materials and continue to build a large beehive out of it. These cells form a pattern resulting in a strong structure which holds together a particular section of the beehive. Here, each cell is independent of the other but it is also correlated with the other cells. This means that damage to one cell does not damage the other cells, so, bees can reconstruct these cells without impacting the complete beehive.

In a Web service we just create a service for an application that can be accessed by web protocols.

Look below for the microservice architecture:

  • Clients – Different users from various devices send requests.
  • Identity Providers – Authenticates user or clients identities and issues security tokens.
  • API Gateway – Handles client requests.
  • Static Content – Houses all the content of the system.
  • Management – Balances services on nodes and identifies failures.
  • Service Discovery – A guide to find the route of communication between microservices.
  • Content Delivery Networks – Distributed network of proxy servers and their data centers.
  • Remote Service – Enables the remote access information that resides on a network of IT devices.

If you wish to learn more about microservices, you can refer to the following blog series & videos:

What Is Microservices | Introduction To Microservice Architecture | Edureka

Happy Learning!:)

Rob Landers, I live and breathe microservices
Answered Apr 5, 2016
Microservices don't necessarily have to be web services, they can serve over file descriptors (pipes, UNIX sockets, etc), through queues, over the network, messages, even email.

I once wrote a microservice that checked an inbox for certain messages and then sent texts over a cell phone modem using AT commands... An ugly hack, but sometimes that's what you have to do.

Microservices do one thing, and they do it well. They'll sometimes have their own datastores, caches, and even their own microservices...

A microservice doesn't have to be restful, it doesn't even have to have an API. A good example is an import sercice... It reads files off a queue and may put new things on another queue and not even need a database.

Another could be an authentication service. It verifies an email/pass over https api and returns a jwt. Other api's can verify the bearer is authorized to access a specific resource.

In other words, A web service may be a microservice, but a microservice doesn't have to be a web service.
